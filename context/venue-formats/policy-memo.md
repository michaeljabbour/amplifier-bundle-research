# Policy Brief / Memo Format Specification

**Venue:** Government, legislative, and NGO policy audiences
**Year:** 2024/2025 (current practice)
**Official Site:** N/A — conventions drawn from legislative style manuals, Harvard Kennedy School / RAND / Brookings / CBO formats

---

## Audience Reality (Read First)

A policy brief is **not** an academic paper. Your reader is a busy decision-maker — a city councilor, agency head, committee staffer, or executive-branch political appointee — who has:

- **5–15 minutes** of reading time for a 2-page memo
- **30 minutes** maximum for a longer brief
- Little patience for academic hedging
- High patience for clear numbers, clear options, and a clear recommendation

Design implications:

1. **Front-load the ask.** The recommendation goes in the title or the first paragraph, not the conclusion.
2. **Bottom-Line-Up-Front (BLUF).** The executive summary must stand alone — readers who only read that page should still know what you want them to do and why.
3. **Optimize for skimmability.** Headings, bullets, and whitespace beat dense prose.
4. **Evidence supports, doesn't dominate.** Citations go in parentheticals and footnotes, not inline with "(Smith, Jones, & Lee, 2023, p. 47)".

If you would not change a policy based on reading just the first page of this brief, it isn't a policy brief — it's a research paper with the wrong hat on.

## Document Structure

Standard sections, in order:

1. **Title** — Action-oriented; includes the ask
   - ✅ "Reduce Pedestrian Fatalities by 30% Through Targeted Speed-Limit Reductions"
   - ❌ "A Study of Pedestrian Fatality Trends in Urban Environments"

2. **Header Block** — Date (prominent), author/office, classification if applicable, "TO / FROM / SUBJECT / DATE" for true memos

3. **Executive Summary** — 150–200 words, one paragraph
   - Problem → Finding → Recommendation → Expected Impact → Call to Action
   - Must be readable standalone

4. **Context / Background** — Why this is on the reader's desk now; what prior actions have been taken; relevant statute, precedent, or policy that constrains options

5. **Key Findings** — 3–5 bullet points, each with a concrete number
   - "City A saw a 22% reduction in fatalities after implementing 25 mph zones (2019–2023)."

6. **Options** — Structured comparison, typically a 3-column table
   - Option A (status quo / do nothing) — always include
   - Option B (moderate intervention)
   - Option C (ambitious intervention)
   - Each option: scope, cost, expected impact, feasibility, political risk

7. **Recommendation** — Clear call to action with rationale; name the option explicitly ("We recommend Option B because...")

8. **Implementation Considerations** — Who does what, by when; legal authority required; budget line items; staffing

9. **Risks & Mitigations** — What could go wrong, and your plan for each

10. **Opposing Views (steel-manned)** — The strongest case against your recommendation, represented fairly, with your rebuttal. Skipping this reads as partisan and erodes credibility with experienced readers.

11. **Evidence Base** — Brief list of key sources with DOI/URL; full citations in appendix

12. **Appendices** — Technical detail, full data tables, methodology notes, extended citations, dissenting-expert letters

Not every section needs to be labeled with these exact headings. Short memos may collapse Implementation Considerations, Risks, and Opposing Views into a single "Considerations" section. Keep the ordering.

## Length Guidance

| Format | Executive Summary | Full Brief | Use When |
|--------|-------------------|------------|----------|
| **One-pager** | N/A (entire doc is the summary) | 1 page | Hearing handout, rapid decision |
| **Short memo** | 150 words | 2–4 pages | Standard agency briefing |
| **Policy brief** | 200 words | 4–8 pages | Think-tank publication, advocacy |
| **Academic policy brief** | 250 words | 15–30 pages | Deeper analysis with methodology appendix; audience includes policy researchers |

Page counts assume single-column, 11–12pt serif, 1" margins (see Format Basics). Going longer than the guidance above means you are writing something other than a policy brief — consider a white paper or report instead.

## Format Basics

### Paper Size
- **Size:** US Letter (8.5" × 11") for US audiences; A4 for international/EU/UN audiences
- **Orientation:** Portrait

### Margins
- **All sides:** 1" (2.54 cm) standard
- **Bound reports:** 1.25" left, 1" other sides
- **Letterhead documents:** Respect the letterhead area — usually 1.5" top margin on page 1

### Layout
- **Columns:** Single column (do not use academic two-column formats)
- **Alignment:** Left-aligned (not justified) — easier to skim
- **Line spacing:** 1.15 or 1.5

### Font
- **Body:** 11–12pt serif (Times New Roman, Cambria, Georgia, Source Serif)
- **Headings:** Same family or a matched sans-serif (Arial, Calibri) at 13–16pt
- **Avoid:** Courier, Comic Sans, purely decorative fonts, tiny type (<10pt)
- **Line length:** 60–80 characters per line (1" margins at 11pt gives ~70)

### Page Elements
- **Page numbers:** Required for anything >1 page; footer, centered or outside corner
- **Header:** Document title (short form) + "DRAFT" / "FINAL" / "CONFIDENTIAL" as applicable
- **Date:** Prominent — on the title and/or header. Policy changes, so the date is decision-relevant.
- **Letterhead:** Use agency/organization letterhead if representing that organization officially

## Evidence & Citation Style

Unlike academic venues, policy briefs use **lightweight in-text references** and save full citations for the appendix.

### In-Text Style
- "According to a 2023 RAND study (Smith et al., 2023)..."
- "The CBO projects a $2.1B cost over 10 years [1]."
- Footnote numbers also acceptable (and common in Congressional style)

### Do NOT Use
- Full APA-style inline with page numbers: `(Smith, Jones, & Lee, 2023, p. 47)` — too noisy
- Bare URLs mid-sentence
- Harvard referencing with detailed elements in the body

### DOI / URL Handling
- Prefer DOI when available, in the appendix citation
- Use shortened URLs in footnotes for readability
- Archive linked content (Wayback Machine) for long-lived references — URLs rot

### Required vs Optional References
- **Required:** Every concrete number, every attribution to a named study or agency, every legal claim
- **Optional:** General-knowledge framings, common-sense observations
- **Forbidden:** Un-sourced statistics — a number without a citation is read as made up

### Citation Format in Appendix
Pick one style and be consistent. For agency work, match the controlling style manual (GPO Style Manual for US federal, House of Commons style for UK). For independent briefs, Chicago author-date or a simplified APA is common.

Example (appendix):
```
Smith, J., Jones, R., & Lee, K. (2023). Speed Limit Reductions and
    Pedestrian Safety in Mid-Sized Cities. RAND Corporation,
    RR-A2345. https://doi.org/10.7249/RRA2345
```

## Voice & Tone

### Active Voice, Direct Address
- ✅ "We recommend the City Council adopt Option B by Q3 2025."
- ❌ "It is recommended that consideration be given to the adoption..."

### Concrete Numbers
- ✅ "15% reduction in pedestrian fatalities"
- ❌ "substantial reduction in pedestrian fatalities"
- ✅ "$2.1M over three years"
- ❌ "a meaningful investment"

### Define Acronyms on First Use
- First use: "Department of Transportation (DOT)"
- Thereafter: "DOT"
- Exception: acronyms expected to be known by the specific audience (e.g., "FBI" in a federal law enforcement brief)

### Avoid Jargon
- Prefer the plain-English term; reserve technical terms for when precision is genuinely needed
- If your reader would need to Google a term, either define it in a footnote or use a different term

### Hedging
- Academic writing rewards hedging ("the data may suggest..."). Policy writing punishes it.
- State what the evidence supports at its actual confidence level, then name the uncertainty separately ("Projected savings: $1–3M depending on adoption rate. 90% CI assumes [X]."). Do **not** bury the confidence interval inside the claim sentence.

### Partisan / Political Tone
- Keep the brief argument-forward but not partisan
- Steel-man opposing views (the Opposing Views section exists for this reason)
- Avoid characterizing opponents' motives; focus on their claims and evidence

## Visualization Conventions

Policy readers are not always scientific readers. Visualization defaults differ from academic figures:

### Every Chart Must Be Interpretable Standalone
- **3-line caption minimum:** what it shows, how to read it, what it means for the policy
- Example: "Figure 1. Pedestrian fatalities per 100,000 residents, 2015–2023, for five mid-sized US cities. Cities that lowered speed limits in 2019 are shown in dark blue; others in gray. The post-2019 decline in dark-blue cities supports the effectiveness of the intervention."

### Chart Type Selection
- ✅ **Bar charts** — for comparisons across discrete categories
- ✅ **Line charts** — for trends over time
- ✅ **Simple maps** — for geographic data (no unnecessary projections or layers)
- ❌ **Complex scientific plots** — box plots with outliers, violin plots, heatmaps with no legend, 3D anything
- ❌ **Dual-axis charts** — read as deceptive by many policy audiences

### Accessibility
- **No color-only encoding** — include patterns, labels, or shapes so the chart works in black-and-white and for color-blind readers
- **Minimum text size:** 10pt at print size
- **Data labels** on bars preferred over requiring the reader to estimate from axis ticks

### Chart-to-Text Ratio
- If a point can be made in a sentence, use a sentence
- If a point requires comparison across 3+ dimensions, use a chart
- Maximum ~1 chart per 2 pages for a short memo; more is fine in a longer brief but each should earn its place

## Audience-Specific Variants

### Legislative Staff (Congressional, state legislature, city council)
- **Shorter** — 1–2 pages preferred
- **Political context explicit** — bill numbers, sponsor names, committee status
- **Coalition analysis** — who supports, who opposes, whose vote is in play
- **Procedural path** — committee → floor → signature timeline

### Executive Branch / Agency Staff
- **More operational** — implementation detail, staff time, IT requirements, legal authority
- **Longer OK** — 4–8 pages standard
- **Reference existing policy** — cite the specific regulation, guidance document, or executive order
- **Budget granularity** — line-item cost projections

### Public-Facing / Constituent Communication
- **Plainest language** — assume general-public reading level (6th–8th grade)
- **No jargon** — define or replace every technical term
- **Narrative framing** — lead with a person-scale example before aggregate stats
- **Call to action appropriate to audience** — "Contact your councilmember" not "Reallocate FY26 line 402.3"

### International / Multilateral (UN, EU, World Bank)
- **British English acceptable** (UK/UN style) or American English — pick one and commit
- **Metric units** always
- **Cite multilateral instruments** by their formal name + abbreviation on first use
- **Expect translation** — favor shorter sentences, avoid idioms

When `target_venue: policy-memo` is set in the preregistration, `venue-formatter` should: (1) load this spec; (2) apply single-column, 1" margins, 11–12pt serif body and 1.15–1.5 line spacing; (3) verify the document has an Executive Summary (150–200 words) that stands alone and contains problem, finding, recommendation, impact, and call-to-action elements; (4) verify every concrete number in the body has an associated citation (flag any un-sourced statistic); (5) rewrite academic-form inline citations to lightweight policy form (`(Smith et al., 2023)` + appendix entry, not `(Smith, Jones, & Lee, 2023, p. 47)`); (6) check that an "Opposing Views" section is present and non-empty — its absence is a credibility risk; (7) check figures have ≥3-line captions and do not rely on color-only encoding; (8) match the appropriate audience variant (legislative, executive, public, international) if that field is set in the preregistration, adjusting length targets accordingly. `venue-formatter` should **not** copy-edit voice/tone — that belongs to `technical-writer` — but should flag obvious academic hedging patterns ("may suggest," "could potentially indicate") for the user to review.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Symptom | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| **Academic paper in memo clothing** | 8-page "brief" with 40 citations and no recommendation until page 7 | Rewrite top-down: recommendation first, evidence second |
| **Missing the ask** | Title and exec summary describe a problem but don't say what to do | Put the action verb in the title |
| **No opposing views** | Brief reads as advocacy, not analysis | Add Opposing Views section; steel-man the strongest case against |
| **Un-sourced numbers** | "Studies show a 30% reduction" with no cite | Every number gets a citation or is cut |
| **Buried recommendation** | Reader finishes and still isn't sure what you want | Put the recommendation in a dedicated section titled "Recommendation" in bold |
| **Uncalibrated confidence** | "This will reduce fatalities by 30%" when the CI is 10–50% | State the point estimate and the CI separately |
| **Color-only charts** | Print-out loses meaning; colorblind readers can't decode | Add patterns, labels, or shapes |
